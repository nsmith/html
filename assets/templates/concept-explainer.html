Fan-out: spread the work, shrink the wait

Instead of one machine grinding through a queue, a coordinator hands pieces of the job to many workers at once. Drag the slider to see how total time falls as workers are added — and where the gains flatten out.

A single worker processing N tasks one after another takes time proportional to N. The idea here is simple: a central hub splits the batch and dispatches it to several workers that run in parallel, so wall-clock time drops toward N / workers — until coordination overhead starts to bite.

4 workers 24 tasks 6.0× time units 2.0× faster than 1 worker

How it works

  1. Split the batch. The hub divides the incoming tasks into roughly equal chunks, one per available worker.
  2. Dispatch in parallel. Each chunk is sent to a worker. All workers start at once rather than waiting their turn.
  3. Collect the results. As workers finish, the hub gathers their output. The slowest chunk sets the finish time.
  4. Watch the curve flatten. Doubling from 1→2 workers nearly halves the time; 8→9 barely moves it. Coordination is not free.

How it compares

Single worker vs. fan-out across the trade-offs that matter.
Dimension Single worker Fan-out
Wall-clock time Grows with the batch — O(N) Falls toward O(N / W)
Coordination cost None — there is nothing to coordinate Split, dispatch, and merge overhead per run
Failure blast radius One failure stalls the whole batch A failed chunk can be retried in isolation
Best when Tasks are tiny or strictly ordered Tasks are independent and plentiful

Key terms

Hub
The coordinator that splits the batch, dispatches chunks, and collects results.
Worker
An independent unit that processes one chunk in parallel with the others.
Fan-out
The act of sending one job out to many workers at the same time.
Diminishing returns
The point where adding more workers stops meaningfully reducing total time because overhead dominates.
